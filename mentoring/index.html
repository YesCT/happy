now that they are participants, regular participants, and exposed to how mentors mentor...
  • Ask
  • In a pinch, when there is a real need, ask (busy office hours, someone could not show up and need a replacement, at an in person sprint, ask for volunteers to do a small thing, something they would-be-mentor *is* comfortable with.
  • give would-be mentors a first mentor task they can succeed at. For example, if a new participant is having trouble setting up webserver in linux, ask a would be mentor that you know is comfortable with that. Dont ask a would-be mentor to help someone do a Mac set up, if they dont already know. Dont ask them to help someone with a call back conversion if they have never themselves done one. It's the people,

It's also the tools!

I'm an example of a contributor who became involved in Drupal because of it's wonderful community. I started my research on open source communities in 2009. At that time I was interested in studying the usability movement in open source, so I picked a number of open srouce communities that care about usability, Drupal was one of them. I started to contact community members and read the bug repositories. After six months I decided to only focus on Drupal. I felt welcomed by the responses that I got from Drupal contributors, I was impressed with the way they worked together to make a better Drupal, and I've been involved in Drupal since then. So, the people and community is important, But, even with a welcoming community, people wont contribute if contributing is difficult. The tools that we use as a community to connect and collaborate have a lot of room for improvement. I think it's time for us to take a closer look at the tools we are using today, find the problems, and improve them.
The most commonly used tool in Drupal community is the issue queue. A good thing about issue queue is that it's fairly easy to use. You just write a comment and click save, or write down a description of an issue and click save. It's easy once you know what to write. But coming to the point that you come up with the right content for the comment you are writing is not so much easy. You often need to go over a process to be able to post a meaningful comment. For example, there are times that you need to post a comment to mediate between other participants with conflicting opinions. In order to do that, you need carefully examine each person's view points, and write a comment to resolve the conflict. But, this may involve talking to them individually on IRC, seeking for an expert opinion on a technical detail, or coming up with a third solution that makes both parties happy. So, contributing to the issue queue is not just writing a comment and clicking save, it's a social process.

But the issue queue doesn't support the social process...

The issue queue has a lot of advantages such as being easy to use, but it doesn't explicitly support the process of coming to consensus and resolving an issue.

Some ideas that people have are actually really good, but then they kind of get lost in the thread. Issue Queues in general aren’t good for keeping track of all of the ideas and the arguments for or against those ideas. The ideas are not batched at the begining, but they are distributed through out the discussion. An important consequence of having the alternatives distributed throughout the discussion is that some may be overlooked or even fade from community memory. As the discussion grows, those who join later or otherwise do not keep up must review the messages to track the alternatives, the arguments for and against them, and the current consensus of the other participants. The common method for acquiring this information is to (re-)read the discussion to date. But, since this is time consuming, some participants will post irrelevant comments that hinder the flow of the discussion.

We need to design for supporting the social process.

We are facing all these problems because the design of the issue queue doesn't fully conider the process of participating in the discussion. Process inclusds understanding the discussion, dealing with conflicts, clarifying confusions, etc. The good news is that there are some theories in Academia that can help us. One of these theories is the consensus building theory. This theory values the same thing that the Drupal community values.

Procid brings consensus building strategies to Drupal.

I've used the theory of consensus building to design an add-on for Drupal issue queue, the add-on is called Procid. Procid is a javascript add-on that attaches to the Drupal issue pages.
It nees minimal input from the users.
You only need to tell me if a comment is an idea or refers to an idea and what is the sentiment.
In return, I'd be able to give you this. Each row presents an idea. An image or text representing the idea, the status of the idea, a list of user defined criteria, and comments related to that idea organized into three categories of supportive, neutral, and contrictive comments. With this you can immediatly see which idea is widely supported, which one has been implemented, etc.

1. Adding a new comment

Give some action items, tell people to pick 1 they can commit to.
It has some cool features. For example, you can add an comment in the context of an idea. You can add a supportive, neutral, or constructive comment. You can also register strong support or strong objection and in that case your comment will be rendered differently.

2. Establishing concrete criteria

Give some action items, tell people to pick 1 they can commit to.
We are already evaluating the solutions proposed in the issue queue with some criteria, but the criteria are defined all over the issue. Procid makes the criteria explicit. You can define a new criteria for evaluating ideas.
You can also rate the ideas based on the criteria and write down the rationale for your rating.

3. Inviting contributors

Give some action items, tell people to pick 1 they can commit to.
There are times that you need to invite new contributors to a discussion, you have to rely on your memory to find contributors. Procid makes it easier for you, it mines through the communities social network and compiles a list of candidates to invite to the discussion based on different attributes.

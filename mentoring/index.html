Habitual Participants
to
Mentor

now that they are participants, regular participants, and exposed to how mentors mentor...
  • Ask
  • In a pinch, when there is a real need, ask (busy office hours, someone could not show up and need a replacement, at an in person sprint, ask for volunteers to do a small thing, something they would-be-mentor *is* comfortable with.
  • give would-be mentors a first mentor task they can succeed at. For example, if a new participant is having trouble setting up webserver in linux, ask a would be mentor that you know is comfortable with that. Dont ask a would-be mentor to help someone do a Mac set up, if they dont already know. Dont ask them to help someone with a call back conversion if they have never themselves done one. Save asking them to do something they dont know the answer to for later.
  • help without being overbearing, overhear them in their first mentoring interactions, stay around the same table, watch the conversation in irc.
  • thank them.
  • say one thing they did well. “you were patient when it took them 15 minutes to find their php.ini” “your explanation of interdiffs really helped that new participant”

It's the people,

but we need tools to support people!

I started my research on open source communities on 2009. At that time I was interested in studying the usability movement in open source, so I picked a number of open srouce communities that care about usability, Drupal was one of them. I started to contact community members and read the bug repositories. After six months I decided to only focus on Drupal. I felt welcomed by the responses that I got from Drupal contributors, I was impressed with the way they worked together to make a better Drupal, and I've been involved in Drupal since then. I'm an example of a contributor who has been engaged in Drupal because of it's wonderful community. But, I found the tools that the community members use rather primitive. Even with a welcoming community, people wont contribute if contributing is difficult. I think it's time for us to as a community to take a closer look at the tools we are using today to contribute to Drupal, find the problems, and improve them.

Changes to the tools will really help.

The most commonly used tool in Drupal community is the issue queue.

The social process matters as much as the result.

This means that the Drupal community have chosen to care more about the process than the result. They don't only care about the final solution, but they care more about the process of coming to that solution. This is why a consensus building process have been chosen rather than voting.

But the issue queue doesn't support the social process...

The issue queue has a lot of advantages such as being easy to use, but it doesn't explicitly support the process of coming to consensus and resolving an issue.

All comments are represented in a similar way...

All comments in a similar way, regardless of who wrote them, the importance of the content, or the strength of the message.

There is no way to track the proposed solutions

Some ideas that people have are actually really good, but then they kind of get lost in the thread.

Or to become aware of arguments for or against the proposed solutions

Issue Queues in general aren’t good for keeping track of all of the ideas and the arguments for or against those ideas.

Or to figure out the current direction of the discussion

It’s often hard to figure out what is the current direction of the discussion. Often it takes getting someone to clarify it. And not everyone would clarify it the same way. If there are two people, and they’re each kind of pushing their own ideas, within a Drupal issue and you go on IRC and ask each of them individually, what’s the current direction?, you’ll get two very different answers.

If we care about the process, we need to design for supporting the process...

We are facing all these problems because the design of the issue queue doesn't fully conider the process of participating in the discussion. Process inclusds understanding the discussion, dealing with conflicts, clarifying confusions, etc.
